Introduction

Bolankian, known natively as Bôulangüneşko, is the dominant language and lingua franca of the island nation of Bolangadesh (Bôulangüneş). The language was first recorded and extensively studied by a team of linguists from the Turkic World Exploration Federation in 1978 CE, during an expedition across the remote southern waters of the Okyanus Continent.

Bolangadesh lies deep within the central expanse of the Okyanus Sea, surrounded by violent tides and dense fog banks thsat have kept it hidden from contact for centuries. Due to this isolation, Bolankian bears almost no resemblance to the languages of neighboring continents. Its syntax and morphology are considered highly agglutinative, with words often formed by stacking multiple particles to refine meaning or emotional tone.

Linguists classify Bolankian as part of the Bolangic language family, a small branch of tongues believed to have descended and diverged from Proto-Bolankian. Its closest relative is Cebanic, spoken on the nearby island of Cebau. Although they share a distant origin, the two languages have evolved separately for over a millennium, resulting in significant differences in vocabulary, pronunciation, and grammar.

Today, Bolankian remains the official and cultural language of Bolangadesh and the Bolankian Islands.

Alphabet and Pronunciation

The Bolankian language doesn't have an indigenous writing system and was originally a purely spoken language. When members of the TWEF first arrived at Bolangadesh, they required a practical way to record the language for study. They ultimately adopted the Turkish alphabet, both because it was familiar to them, and because Bolangadesh shared several phonetic similarities with Turkish.

Vowel Harmony

Bolankian employs a system of vowel harmony, where vowels within a word harmonize to be either front or back vowels. This affects the choice of suffixes and inflections added to words.

Front Vowels e, i, ü, ê, î, é, êi, eh, ih
Back Vowels a, o, u, ö, â, ô, û, ôu, ah, oh, uh,
Neutral Vowels ı, ë, ëh, eu, e, eh

Note:

The letters q, w, and x are not used in native Bolankian words, but may appear in loanwords. Stress in Bolankian usually falls on the first syllable of a word, but there are exceptions based on word origin and structure. The letter ğ is silent when it appears at the beginning of a word. The letter h is silent when it appears at the end of a word. "Eu" is very tricky and changes per word. For example, the name "Mogeul" is pronounced [mogl̩] while the word "eu" (meaning "I/me") is pronounced [ʊ]. It can also be pronounced [ɨ] in some words. The best way to learn it is through practice and exposure.

The Basics

Sentence Order

Bolankian does not follow a strict sentence order because of its agglutinative system having case markers, but the most common and preferred order is Subject-Object-Verb (SOV). The one rule in Bolankian sentence structure is that the tense/aspect/mood marker always comes at the end of the sentence.

Case Markers

Like I've said before, Bolankian is a highly agglutinative language. One of the most important aspects of this is the use of case markers. Case markers are suffixes that are added to the end of nouns to indicate their grammatical role in a sentence.

The most common cases and case markers in Bolankian are:

  1. Nominative (subject): no marker
  2. Accusative (direct object): -leut / -eut
  3. Dative (indirect object): -on / -en / -ğon / -ğen / -n
  4. Genitive (possession): -nın / -onan / -nin / -ünin
  5. Locative (location): -da / -de / -şa / -şe / -ça / -çe
  6. Ablative (movement away from): -dan / -den / -şan / -şen / -çan / -çen
  7. Instrumental (means by which action is performed): -le / -e / -yla / -ye / -la / -a

Agreement

In Bolankian, verbs must agree with their subjects in both number and person. This is achieved through the use of verb endings that correspond to the subject's characteristics. Agreement also depends on negation. If the subject is negated (such as "no one" or "nothing"), the verb also has to be negated. Verbs and their subjects also should have the same vowel harmony. Adjectives do not need to agree with the nouns they modify.
